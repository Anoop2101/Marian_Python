Aditya Birla Group: Workforce Compensation Analytics

CASE STUDY 1 — HR ANALYTICS & WORKFORCE PLANNING

🏢 Organization Overview

Aditya Birla Group is one of India's largest multinational conglomerates with operations in 36 countries and a workforce of over 140,000 employees spanning metals, textiles, cement, financial services, and telecommunications. The Group HR & People Analytics Division is responsible for driving data-informed workforce strategies across all business units.

📁 Business Context

The Compensation Planning & Analytics Team manages employee data across departments including Engineering, Sales, HR, and Finance. Their mandate is to identify salary disparities, track attrition trends, and provide actionable insights to the Chief Human Resources Officer (CHRO) before the annual compensation review cycle.

The dataset contains critical workforce attributes: Employee ID, Name, Department, Monthly Salary, Years of Experience, and Attrition Status.

📖 The Story Behind the Analysis

The CHRO noticed that certain business units were experiencing disproportionately high turnover while others retained talent consistently. Without a systematic aggregation of employee data, it was impossible to determine whether salary, experience, or department culture was driving the attrition problem.

The People Analytics team was tasked with building a Compensation & Retention Dashboard using Python and Pandas — moving from intuition-based HR decisions to a data-driven workforce strategy.

⚠️ Key Business Challenges

  • 🔍 Pay Disparity: Significant salary gaps exist across departments, making it difficult to assess compensation fairness.
  • 📉 Attrition Risk: Departments with lower average salaries show consistently higher turnover, increasing recruitment and training costs.
  • 🧩 Data Fragmentation: Employee metrics (salary, experience, attrition) are analysed in isolation — no unified view exists for leadership decision-making.

🎯 Project Objective

The objective of this project is to perform a Workforce Compensation Audit using Pandas — applying univariable, bivariable, and multivariable aggregation techniques to uncover salary trends, department-level disparities, and attrition patterns across the organization.

🛠️ Project Requirements

Univariable analysis — Single column summaries

R1 — Summarise the overall salary distribution
Calculate the average, minimum, maximum, and spread of salaries across all 8 employees combined to establish a company-wide pay baseline.

R2 — Count total attrition across the company
Count how many employees left versus how many stayed, and calculate the overall attrition rate as a percentage using only the Attrition column.

R3 — Analyse experience distribution of the workforce
Find the average, median, minimum, and maximum years of experience across all employees to understand overall workforce seniority.

Bivariable analysis — Department-level comparisons

R4 — Compare average salary across departments
Group employees by department and calculate the average salary for each group to identify which teams are paid the most and least.

R5 — Compare average experience across departments
Group employees by department and calculate the average years of experience per group to check whether lower-paid departments also have less experienced staff.

R6 — Measure attrition rate per department
Group employees by department and calculate the proportion of staff who left within each group to identify which departments are most affected by turnover.

R7 — Count headcount per department
Group employees by department and count the number of staff in each group to provide context for all other metrics — a 50% attrition rate in a 2-person team is far more severe than in a larger one.

Multivariable analysis — Cross-column insights

R8 — Build a department-level HR dashboard summary
Group by department and calculate four metrics at once — average salary, average experience, total headcount, and total payroll — in a single aggregation table for the core HR reporting view.

R9 — Profile employees who left versus those who stayed
Group by both department and attrition status together, then compare average salary and experience of leavers against retainers within each department to understand who is leaving and why.

R10 — Apply different summary measures to different columns
Group by department and apply a tailored summary to each column — mean and standard deviation for Salary, mean and maximum for Experience, and a count for Attrition — all in one operation to expose intra-department inequality.

📊 Univariable Analysis in Python

📌 What is Univariable Analysis?

Univariable Analysis means analysing only one variable (one column) at a time.

It helps us understand:

  • distribution of data,
  • central tendency,
  • minimum and maximum values,
  • and overall behaviour of a single column.

In HR Analytics, univariable analysis helps understand:

  • salary distribution,
  • employee experience levels,
  • attrition count,
  • department-wise employee counts, etc.

📂 Example from Employee Dataset

In this dataset:

ColumnMeaning
SalaryEmployee salary
ExperienceEmployee experience
AttritionWhether employee left or stayed

If we analyse only:

  • Salary column → Univariable Analysis
  • Experience column → Univariable Analysis
  • Attrition column → Univariable Analysis

Because we are analysing only one variable at a time.

📊 Requirement 1 — Summarise the Overall Salary Distribution

📌 Business Requirement

The HR department wants to understand the company-wide salary structure. They need to calculate:

  • Average Salary
  • Minimum Salary
  • Maximum Salary
  • Salary Spread

This helps management understand the overall employee compensation pattern.

🐍 Python Code

In [1]:
# Average Salary
average_salary = df["Salary"].mean()

# Minimum Salary
minimum_salary = df["Salary"].min()

# Maximum Salary
maximum_salary = df["Salary"].max()

# Salary Spread
salary_spread = maximum_salary - minimum_salary

print("Average Salary :", average_salary)
print("Minimum Salary :", minimum_salary)
print("Maximum Salary :", maximum_salary)
print("Salary Spread :", salary_spread)

📖 Explanation of the Code

1️⃣ mean()
df["Salary"].mean() — Calculates the average salary of all employees. It adds all salaries and divides by total employees.

2️⃣ min()
df["Salary"].min() — Finds the lowest salary in the company.

3️⃣ max()
df["Salary"].max() — Finds the highest salary in the company.

4️⃣ Salary Spread
maximum_salary - minimum_salary — Calculates the difference between highest and lowest salary. This shows salary variation inside the organization.

📊 Explanation of the Outcome

Suppose the output is:

Average Salary : 55125
Minimum Salary : 38000
Maximum Salary : 85000
Salary Spread : 47000

✅ Business Understanding

  • The average employee salary is ₹55,125.
  • The minimum salary is ₹38,000.
  • The maximum salary is ₹85,000.
  • The salary gap inside the company is ₹47,000.

This helps HR understand compensation distribution, identify salary variation, and maintain fair pay structures.

📊 Requirement 2 — Count Total Attrition Across the Company

📌 Business Requirement

# Employees Left

2️⃣ Employees Stayed
(df["Attrition"] == "No").sum() — Counts employees who stayed in the company.

3️⃣ Total Employees
len(df) — Calculates total employees in the dataset.

4️⃣ Attrition Rate
(employees_left / total_employees) * 100 — Calculates percentage of employees who left the company.

Employees Stayed : 5

📊 Bivariable Analysis in Python

📌 What is Bivariable Analysis?

Bivariable Analysis means analysing two variables (two columns) together to find relationships between them.

It helps us understand:

  • how one variable changes with another,
  • comparisons between groups,
  • and patterns across categories.

In HR Analytics, bivariable analysis helps understand:

  • average salary per department,
  • experience level per department,
  • attrition rate per department, etc.

📊 Multivariable Analysis in Python

📌 What is Multivariable Analysis?

Multivariable Analysis means analysing three or more variables (multiple columns) at the same time to uncover deeper patterns and relationships.

It helps us understand:

  • how multiple variables interact with each other,
  • complex group-level patterns,
  • and combined insights across several dimensions.

In HR Analytics, multivariable analysis helps understand:

  • salary, experience, and headcount together per department,
  • leaver profiles combining department, salary, and attrition,
  • and applying different summary measures to different columns.

📂 Example from Employee Dataset

In this dataset:

AnalysisColumns Used
Department DashboardDepartment + Salary + Experience + Headcount
Leaver ProfileDepartment + Salary + Experience + Attrition
Tailored SummarySalary (mean,std) + Experience (mean,max) + Attrition (count)

Because we are analysing multiple columns simultaneously to build a complete HR intelligence view.

📊 Requirement 8 — Build a Department-Level HR Dashboard Summary

📌 Business Requirement

The HR leadership needs a single dashboard view showing multiple metrics per department — average salary, total experience, headcount, and total payroll.

🐍 Python Code

In [8]:
# Department dashboard
dashboard = df.groupby("Department").agg({
    "Salary": "mean",
    "Experience": "sum",
    "EmpID": "count"
})

print(dashboard)

📖 Explanation of the Code

1️⃣ agg() with Dictionary
df.groupby("Department").agg({...}) — Applies different aggregation functions to different columns in one operation.

2️⃣ Metrics Calculated
— "Salary": "mean" → Average salary per department
— "Experience": "sum" → Total years of experience in each department
— "EmpID": "count" → Number of employees per department

📊 Explanation of the Outcome

Suppose the output is:

Department Average Salary (₹) Total Experience (Years) Total Employees
Engineering78,500122
Finance60,000112
HR43,50052
Sales38,50062

✅ Business Understanding

  • Engineering's total payroll (₹1,57,000) is double Sales (₹77,000).
  • HR has the lowest total experience (5 years) and second-lowest pay.
  • Management gets a clear picture of where the compensation budget is concentrated.
