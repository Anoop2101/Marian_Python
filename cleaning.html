Data Understanding & Cleaning

Join us as we transform a raw, messy HR dataset into a polished executive report, step-by-step.

The Context

The Reality of Business Data

In a real-world boardroom, decisions are only as good as the data behind them. Most raw data is "Dirty" — it has missing values, duplicates, and errors. An MBA analyst's first job is to fix these traps.

Analysts spend 80% of their time cleaning data, and only 20% analyzing it!
Step 1: The Toolkit

Arming Ourselves with Libraries

We start by loading Pandas (our data table expert) and NumPy (our math genius). These tools give Python the "superpowers" to handle massive spreadsheets.

In [1]:
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
Step 2: The Handshake

Importing your First Dataset

We connect our Python script to the HR_Data.csv file. This creates a DataFrame — an interactive, smart table in computer memory.

In [2]:
df = pd.read_csv("HR_Data.csv")
Analyst Tip: A "DataFrame" is simply Python's version of an Excel table. It can hold millions of rows without slowing down.
Step 3: The Deep Audit

Understanding the Data Skeleton

Before touching anything, we inspect the data structure. How many employees? What are their salary formats? Where are the gaps?

In [3]:
df.info()
Total Rows: 1000 | Data Columns: 5
Salary: float64 (Numbers)
JoiningDate: object (Needs Cleaning)
Step 4: The Visual Scan

A Quick Peek at the Rows

Good analysts never trust a summary alone. We peek at random rows to see if anything looks "off" using sample().

In [4]:
df.sample(2)

Warning! Sarah's salary is NaN (Not a Number). This will break our total calculations later!
Step 5 & 6: Mending the Gaps

Handling Missing Values

We decide to fill the empty salary gaps with the Average (Mean) salary of the rest of the company so Sarah is still included in our report.

In [6]:
df["Salary"] = df["Salary"].fillna(df["Salary"].mean())
Why Mean? In typical numeric data, the average is the safest guess. If the data had extreme outliers (like a CEO's $10M salary), we would use the Median instead to avoid skewing our results.
Step 7: Departmental Census

Univariate Analysis

We check the concentration of staff. Which department rules the office? We use value_counts() for this census.

In [7]:
df["Dept"].value_counts()
Sales: 450 | IT: 350 | HR: 200

Visual Insight: Sales dominates with 45% of total headcount.

Step 8 & 9: The Ghost Purge

Removing Duplicate Records

Duplicates are repeated entries that skew your profit/loss numbers. We scan and delete them instantly.

In [8]:
print(df.duplicated().sum())
df = df.drop_duplicates()
Duplicates Found: 12 | Status: Purged.
Step 10-12: The Professional Polish

Renaming and Type Tuning

We rename "Dept" to "Department" for professional readability and convert raw dates into Python-friendly DateTime objects.

In [11]:
df.rename(columns={"Dept":"Department"}, inplace=True)
df["JoiningDate"] = pd.to_datetime(df["JoiningDate"])
Step 13 & 14: Creating Value

Data Transformation (KPIs)

Cleaning is over; now we create insights! We calculate a 10% Corporate Bonus for every employee to prepare for the annual budget review.

In [14]:
df["Bonus"] = df["Salary"] * 0.10
